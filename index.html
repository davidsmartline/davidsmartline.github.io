Hello,this is Smart Line's open ideas! My journey into robotics didn't begin in a laboratory. It began on a factory floor. Before coming to the United States, I spent four years working as a mechanic in an automobile factory. During that period, I designed and built industrial equipment including grinding machines, broaching machines, and testing systems. That experience taught me that real innovation starts with real manufacturing problems. In the 1980s, I was selected through China's national scholarship program to study in the United States. Later, while pursuing my PhD at New York University, I worked on multi-axis direct-drive robotic systems, which became my formal introduction to robotics. Shortly after graduation, I joined a project to automate the loading of large automotive glass panels. At that time, the work was extremely dangerous and many factories still relied on manual labor. During on-site testing, I was seriously injured when a glass panel fell on my hand. Fortunately, the project succeeded, and the resulting robotic system helped improve safety throughout the industry. Our team received the industry's highest innovation award. That experience shaped my philosophy. Technology matters only when it solves important real-world problems. Over the next two decades, I continued developing industrial automation systems and building intellectual property, resulting in more than 200 patents in worldwide. Today, I believe robotics faces a challenge similar to what computing faced decades ago. AI is advancing rapidly, but robot bodies remain largely unchanged. Most robotics companies focus on software, sensors, or AI models. We are focused on something different: creating fundamentally new robotic body architectures. Our nonlinear scissor-based robotic structures enable ultra-long reach, energy efficiency，simple Kinematic equation algorithm， lightweight designs, modular expansion, and entirely new forms of physical intelligence. In short, we are not trying to build a better robot arm for AI embeded robots. We are trying to redefine what a robot body can be. That is the mission of Smart Line Robotics.

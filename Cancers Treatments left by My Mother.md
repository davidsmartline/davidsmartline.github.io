Cancers Treatments left by My Mother

David Tang 

March，2013

My mother is a doctor who has been practicing medicine for over 50 years, treating many patients and curing numerous diseases. Among them, there are quite a few cancer patients, each with their own stories. When she was in her 60s, she was diagnosed with advanced lymphoma. Through a combination of chemotherapy and her own medical expertise, she managed to heal herself and even presented an academic report titled "Questioning Symptoms" at Hunan/Yale Medical College. I want to write down a bit of her experiences that I know so that others can benefit from them.

1. Let the patients speak out.
My mother once said that most cancer patients develop the disease after experiencing significant emotional blows and prolonged worries. Long-term mental stress without relief leads to illness. When worries become illnesses, the pain goes deep and one can't cry it out. That's why it's important to let patients speak out and patiently listen. Here, I recall that in the United States, cancer patients are often brought together for group discussions, allowing each patient to express themselves, which has proven to be very effective.

2. Inquiry.
We not only ask about their medical conditions, but also inquire about their lives, work environments, and their families. Everyday matters are also included in the conversation. Furthermore, we ask about their past experiences, and patients are often willing to reminisce about their past, finding joy in sharing their memories. This helps improve the patients' mental state.

3. Listen.
With great care and compassion, we listen attentively. A good doctor can hear even the slightest sound from the patient, even their breathing, very clearly. We listen with empathy, putting ourselves in their shoes. We nod and acknowledge, often saying "yes." The ability to smoothly engage in the process of asking, speaking, and listening is a skill my mother has as a doctor.

The process of asking, speaking, and listening is a form of treatment itself. I often hear my mother's patients say, "Your mother hasn't even treated my illness yet, but talking to her has already cured half of it."

4. Care.
My mother once said that "love" can enhance a person's immune system, and with love, one becomes stronger in resisting diseases. Care can be a form of treatment. Let me share a story of how my mother used care to treat an illness that I personally experienced:

When I was in junior high school, my father was hospitalized with lung disease. My mother mobilized almost all our relatives and friends to visit him in batches, and she told me, "No matter how busy you are with your studies and schoolwork, go see your father as soon as possible." When I entered the hospital room, I saw all the doctors and nurses from my mother's department there. They said, "Tang, your precious son is here to see you!" My father, reluctant to give me face, said, "I actually don't like my son; I prefer my twin daughters." I replied, "Although Dad doesn't like me, I love you. It's unrequited love." Everyone laughed. Miraculously, my father's illness quickly improved.

When a patient learns that they have a serious illness, it is a harsh reality for them. They need even more care, but in today's busy society, everyone tends to overlook this kind of care. It needs to be reminded and promoted. Recently, I read a book on interpersonal relationships in the United States, and it said, "If not many people come to you while you're alive, you can ask those who have passed away to come." Indeed, many deceased individuals are still alive in our hearts and can be invited.

5. Help the patients let go.
Whenever my mother starts talking with a patient, it never seems to end. Often, the whole family waits for her to come back for dinner, and whenever we find her, she is engrossed in conversation with a patient. She talks about the causes and pathologies of the disease, helping the patients let go of the burdens in their lives, advising them not to dwell on their difficulties, and encouraging them to maintain a sense of calm and tranquility. This provides a sense of liberation for their spirits, allowing them to walk their chosen path and do what they love. I often hear my mother humming the song "Goodbye, Song" from the novel "Dream of the Red Chamber," which helps patients relax and brings warmth to their souls.

Postscript:
"Why bury bones in the Mulberry Grove? Green mountains are everywhere in the human world." It's Qingming Festival again, and it has been five years since my mother left us.
I remember when I was in elementary school, my younger sister asked my mother an interesting question, "Mom, if you can't even slaughter a chicken, how can you perform surgery on people?"
My mother replied, "When I think that making one incision can save two lives!" Indeed, my mother once made an incision that saved three lives—a pregnant woman and a pair of twins, and a family.
Later, both of my younger sisters and one older sister became doctors. Several of their children also became doctors. Now, the tradition of practicing medicine has passed down through four generations of our family, which I hope can bring solace to your spirit in heaven.
